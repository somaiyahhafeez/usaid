USAID's uncertain future risks global aid to various countries, especially its largest beneficiary, Ukraine

President Trump's sweeping cuts to USAID funding jeopardize billions in international assistance, with the agency's largest beneficiary facing the greatest impact.

By Somaiyah Hafeez | Published March 5, 2025

In the months since returning to the White House, President Donald Trump has implemented a series of controversial policies through a record 26 executive orders signed on his first day in office, including the elimination of birthright citizenship and restrictions on gender identity recognition.

However, perhaps the most consequential decision for global humanitarian efforts has been the administration's dramatic reduction in foreign assistance funding, particularly targeting the U.S. Agency for International Development (USAID). According to recent reports, the administration has terminated more than 90% of USAID's foreign assistance awards as part of a government reorganization program led by Trump ally Elon Musk through the newly formed Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE).

Key Development

The scope of USAID cuts became clear in early February when the administration announced plans to retain fewer than 300 staff out of the agency's global workforce of more than 10,000 employees, effectively dismantling its operational capacity worldwide.

As the chart above illustrates, USAID's annual global commitments have significantly changed over the last two decades. The agency has been the cornerstone of American foreign assistance, managing 61% of the more than $71 billion in foreign aid allocated in 2023, according to ForeignAssistance.gov. These substantial commitments have funded critical programs addressing health crises, food insecurity, economic development, and humanitarian emergencies worldwide.

The path to dismantling USAID began in January when Secretary of State Marco Rubio suspended U.S. foreign aid for 90 days following a presidential executive order. By March, the administration completed its review, resulting in the termination of almost all USAID programs despite widespread diplomatic concerns.

Analysis of USAID funding patterns reveals a dramatic shift in priorities following Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. As shown in the line chart above, while funding to most countries remained relatively stable or even declined over the past decade, Ukraine experienced an unprecedented surge, receiving more than $30 billion since the conflict began—a 96% increase compared to pre-war levels.

This stands in contrast to other conflict zones. Afghanistan, for example, saw its aid package drastically reduced following the U.S. withdrawal and Taliban takeover in 2021.

The detailed breakdown in the table above illustrates how dramatically USAID's geographic priorities have evolved. Ukraine now dominates the recipient list, having displaced traditional aid recipients like Jordan, Ethiopia, and South Sudan. Of particular note is the shift in funding categories: from 2022-2024, approximately $28 billion—over 90% of Ukraine's total aid package—was allocated to "governance" initiatives aimed at supporting institution building, democratic reforms, and macroeconomic stability in the conflict zone.

Experts warn that the abrupt termination of USAID programs could have cascading effects on global stability. Programs providing emergency relief, health services, food security, and education in conflict-affected regions face immediate peril. Initiatives combating infectious diseases, supporting refugee populations, and promoting sustainable development also hang in the balance.

The reduction in U.S. foreign assistance threatens these critical humanitarian programs, leaving millions without essential support and deepening global crises, ultimately undermining efforts to alleviate suffering in some of the world's most vulnerable regions.
